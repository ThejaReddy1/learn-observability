The OTel Collector:
A Complete Guide

What the Collector is, how it works, how to configure it — and the hard lessons our team learned running it in production at scale.

Thejasekhar Reddy.G ·  April 4, 2026

01 — What It IsThe Collector Is Your Telemetry Router

The OpenTelemetry Collector is a standalone service that sits between your applications and your observability backends. Your apps send telemetry to the Collector, the Collector processes it, and then routes it to wherever you need it to go.

One liner

Receive → Process → Export

That's the entire job of the Collector. Everything else — the config, the components, the pipelines — is just the details of how you configure those three steps.

OTel Collector — Receive · Process · Export

You don't have to use a Collector. You can send telemetry directly from your apps to a backend. But once you're running more than a handful of services, the Collector becomes essential — it gives you a central place to control what data goes where, how it's processed, and at what cost.

02 — Core ArchitectureReceivers, Processors, and Exporters

Every Collector pipeline is made of three types of components:

📥
Receivers
How telemetry gets into the Collector. Common receivers: OTLP (from OTel SDKs), Prometheus (scraping), Jaeger, Zipkin, hostmetrics (CPU/memory/disk from the host). You can have multiple receivers feeding the same pipeline.
⚙️
Processors
Transformations applied to telemetry before it's exported. Batching (group records for efficiency), filtering (drop what you don't need), attribute manipulation (add, remove, rename), sampling (reduce trace volume). This is where most of the power lives.
📤
Exporters
Where telemetry goes out. OTLP (to any OTel-compatible backend), Prometheus remote write, Jaeger, logging (to stdout for debugging), and dozens of vendor-specific exporters for Datadog, Grafana Cloud, Elastic, etc.

You wire these together into pipelines — one pipeline for metrics, one for traces, one for logs. A single pipeline can fan out to multiple exporters, letting you send the same data to multiple backends simultaneously.

03 — Two Distributionsotelcol vs otelcol-contrib

The Collector comes in two flavors:

otelcol

The core distribution. Includes only the officially supported, stable components. Smaller binary, tighter security surface. Good for production if the core components cover your needs.

otelcol-contrib

Includes everything in core plus community-contributed components — 100+ receivers, processors, and exporters. Much larger set of integrations. Most teams start here and trim down later.

Our take

Start with otelcol-contrib while you're figuring out your setup. Once your pipelines are stable, consider building a custom distribution with only what you actually use — it reduces attack surface and binary size significantly.

04 — Deployment PatternsAgent vs Gateway

There are two main ways to deploy the Collector, and most mature setups use both:

Agent Mode (on every host)

Run one Collector instance per host or pod, co-located with your applications. The agent receives telemetry from local apps and does lightweight processing — adding host metadata, batching — before forwarding to a central gateway. Low latency, no single point of failure.

Gateway Mode (centralized)

A small fleet of Collectors that receive from all your agents, do heavier processing — tail-based sampling, aggregation, routing to multiple backends — and export to final destinations. Acts as the control point for your entire telemetry pipeline.

Our experience

The agent-gateway pattern gives you the best of both worlds — resilience at the edge, control at the center. The downside: you now have a fleet of agents to manage. That's where a proper agent management layer becomes essential.

05 — Config WalkthroughA Real Production Config

Here's a simplified but realistic Collector config showing the structure:

config.yaml — the basic structure

Receivers → Processors → Exporters → Service

Every config has these four top-level sections. You define your components under each section, then wire them into pipelines under service.pipelines. Components that aren't referenced in a pipeline don't run.

1
receivers: — define your inputs
Common: otlp (grpc + http), hostmetrics (scrapers for cpu, memory, disk, network), prometheus (scrape existing Prometheus endpoints).
2
processors: — transform in flight
Almost always include batch (essential for performance), memory_limiter (prevents OOM kills), resourcedetection (auto-adds host/cloud metadata).
3
exporters: — define your outputs
otlp or otlphttp to send to any OTel-compatible backend. debug during development to print to stdout. prometheusremotewrite for Prometheus/Grafana setups.
4
service.pipelines: — wire it all together
Separate pipelines for traces, metrics, and logs. Each specifies which receivers feed it, which processors transform it, and which exporters receive it.

Here's what a real production-grade agent config looks like — the kind you'd run on every host, co-located with your application:

config.yaml — production agent config (otelcol-contrib)
# ── RECEIVERS ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
receivers:

  otlp:                          # accept telemetry from your app via SDK
    protocols:
      grpc:
        endpoint: 0.0.0.0:4317
      http:
        endpoint: 0.0.0.0:4318

  hostmetrics:                   # scrape CPU, memory, disk, network from the host
    collection_interval: 30s
    scrapers:
      cpu:
      memory:
      disk:
      network:
      load:

# ── PROCESSORS ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
processors:

  memory_limiter:                # safety valve — always first in every pipeline
    check_interval: 1s
    limit_mib:      400
    spike_limit_mib: 100

  resourcedetection:             # auto-detect host, OS, cloud metadata
    detectors: [env, system, docker]
    override:   false

  resource:                      # add static deployment metadata
    attributes:
      - key:    deployment.environment
        value:  "production"
        action: insert
      - key:    team.name
        value:  "platform-eng"
        action: insert

  batch:                         # group records before export — always last before exporter
    send_batch_size:    8192
    send_batch_max_size: 16384
    timeout:            200ms

# ── EXPORTERS ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
exporters:

  otlp:                          # forward to a central gateway collector
    endpoint: https://gateway.internal:4317
    compression: gzip            # always enable — reduces payload by ~80%
    retry_on_failure:
      enabled: true
      max_elapsed_time: 300s

  debug:                         # stdout output — disable in production, useful in staging
    verbosity: basic

# ── EXTENSIONS ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
extensions:
  health_check:                  # liveness probe for Kubernetes
    endpoint: 0.0.0.0:13133
  zpages:                        # debug UI at localhost:55679

# ── SERVICE ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
service:
  extensions: [health_check, zpages]

  pipelines:

    traces:
      receivers:   [otlp]
      processors:  [memory_limiter, resourcedetection, resource, batch]
      exporters:   [otlp]

    metrics:
      receivers:   [otlp, hostmetrics]
      processors:  [memory_limiter, resourcedetection, resource, batch]
      exporters:   [otlp]

    logs:
      receivers:   [otlp]
      processors:  [memory_limiter, resourcedetection, resource, batch]
      exporters:   [otlp]

  telemetry:                     # collector's own internal metrics — scrape on :8888
    metrics:
      level:   detailed
      address: 0.0.0.0:8888
Key things to notice

memory_limiter is always first in every processor chain — before any other processing. batch is always last before the exporter. resourcedetection + resource together give you automatic host metadata plus custom deployment attributes on every span. gzip compression on the OTLP exporter — never skip this in production. And service.telemetry exposes the Collector's own metrics on port 8888 so you can monitor the monitor.

06 — Hard LessonsWhat We Learned Running This in Production

From the trenches

We've run OTel Collectors across multiple environments. Here's what bit us — so it doesn't bite you.

Always set memory_limiter first in your processor chain

If your Collector gets hit with a spike of telemetry, it will try to buffer everything in memory — and get OOM-killed. The memory_limiter processor drops data before that happens. Put it first in every pipeline's processor list. Yes, you'll lose some data during spikes. That's better than losing your entire Collector.

Batch processor is not optional

Without batching, every span and metric is exported individually. That hammers your backends and kills performance. The batch processor groups records and sends them together. Always include it, and tune the timeout and send_batch_size for your workload.

Your Collector needs to be monitored too

The Collector exposes its own metrics on port 8888. Scrape them. Watch for otelcol_processor_dropped_metric_points and otelcol_exporter_send_failed_metric_points — those are the canaries that tell you your pipeline is struggling. If you're not watching your Collector, you're flying blind about your observability system.

Config drift is a real problem at scale

When you're running dozens of Collector agents, configs drift. Someone SSHes in, makes a "quick fix", and now one agent is running differently from the others. You won't know until something breaks. Tracking configs centrally — versioned and auditable — is the right answer to this problem.
